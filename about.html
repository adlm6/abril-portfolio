About Me

The short version: I like building things that hold up under real use.

I'm a third-year student at Yale studying Computer Science and Economics, which is a roundabout way of saying I'm interested in how systems — whether they're distributed databases or labor markets — actually behave once real people start using them. Over the past two years I've spent my summers and semesters embedded on engineering teams, shipping features that touched real users rather than just problem sets.

My coursework leans into algorithms, systems, and econometrics, but most of what I know about building software came from internships: debugging production issues at 2am, arguing about API design in code review, and learning that the hardest part of most projects is the requirements, not the code. I care about writing software that's readable six months later, and about backing decisions — technical or economic — with actual data instead of vibes.

Outside of class and work, I edit film photography and run economic models on things that don't need it, purely for fun.
